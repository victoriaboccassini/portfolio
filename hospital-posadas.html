Designing for Equity:
Affordable Medical Devices for Low-Resource Contexts

Pediatric Standing Frame Research | Hospital Posadas, Buenos Aires | 2008

KEY OUTCOME
Functional standing frame prototype designed for small/medium-scale production in hospital's maker-space, enabling affordable rehabilitation devices for pediatric patients with neurological conditions

Project Overview

Partnership between University of Buenos Aires and Hospital Nacional Profesor Alejandro Posadas—a high-complexity national public hospital (#10 QS Latin America) providing free care across Buenos Aires Province. Many low-income pediatric patients with cerebral palsy lack access to rehabilitation devices, compromising their health outcomes. The hospital commissioned the university to address this gap through in-house manufacturing capability.

Why This Project?

Inequitable Access to Rehabilitation

Hospital Posadas serves a large patient population across Buenos Aires Province, including suburban and rural areas. Many low-income pediatric patients lacked access to rehabilitation services and medical devices—commercial standing frames were financially out of reach for families earning minimum wage.

High Device Abandonment Rates

Even when families obtained devices, abandonment was common. Therapists reported that difficulty of use by single caregivers and poor fit to home environments led families to stop using devices despite clinical benefits.

Clinical-Only Design Paradigm

Existing solutions designed for institutional settings ignored home realities and children's emotional/social needs. Commercial devices were bulky, institutional-looking equipment with no consideration for family life, spatial constraints, or child experience.

Research Methods

Expert Interviews

5 healthcare professionals | Moderated, semi-structured
Why this method:

Needed clinical requirements AND real-world adoption barriers before designing. Healthcare professionals prescribe these devices and observe long-term usage patterns and abandonment factors.

Key finding: Difficulty in device placement by single caregiver is #1 reason for abandonment, even when clinically beneficial

Caregiver Interviews + Site Visits

3 primary caregivers | Hospital + 1 home visit | Moderated
Why this method:

Caregivers are the actual operators of the device. Site visits (including one home visit) revealed environmental constraints and daily reality that couldn't be captured in hospital interviews alone.

Key finding: 4x3m living spaces with entire family meant portability/storage was as critical as clinical efficacy—common trap in medical device design is prioritizing clinical needs over home realities

Observational Research

Pediatric patients at Hospital Posadas
Why this method:

Children couldn't participate in interviews due to verbal/cognitive limitations from neurological conditions. Observation was the only method to understand their interaction with the device and social integration needs.

Key finding: Children's need for 360° visibility and play capability while standing was critical for peer interaction—purely clinical designs miss the social development aspect

Key Insights → Design Requirements

Single caregiver operates alone: Simplify placement mechanism, reduce physical effort required
Commercial devices unaffordable: Design for low-cost, small-scale manufacturing—significantly cheaper than market alternatives
Extreme spatial constraints (4x3m homes): Collapsible design, compact storage, doorway-width footprint
Institutional aesthetic inappropriate for homes: Child-friendly design with warm appearance vs. bulky medical equipment
Children need social integration: 360° visibility, integrated play/activity surface at peer eye-level
Caregiver burnout common: Prioritize ease of use over additional clinical features to prevent abandonment
Personalized medical requirements: Adjustable, modular components for different pathologies

Impact

Clinical Validation

Prototype met all therapeutic requirements validated by 5 healthcare professionals at Hospital Posadas

User Testing Success

3 caregivers successfully operated device independently during testing—primary success metric

Low-Cost, Small-Scale Manufacturing

Design feasible for small-batch production in hospital's maker-space—no industrial-scale manufacturing required. Estimated 60-70% lower cost than commercial alternatives.

Context Adaptation

Single-caregiver operation and home environment adaptation resulted in immediate family interest and willingness to adopt the device—addressing the primary abandonment barrier

Child-Centered Design

Higher acceptance from children—caregivers observed increased cooperation during therapy sessions due to the approachable, less intimidating appearance

Reflection & Learnings

What Worked Well

Starting with expert interviews gave us clinical grounding before user research. The home visit was invaluable—seeing the actual 4x3m space completely changed our design priorities.

What I'd Do Differently

More caregiver interviews (we only had 3) and longitudinal observation following families over weeks would reveal usage patterns we couldn't see in single sessions.

Methodological Decision I'm Most Proud Of

Doing extensive contextual research on housing and socioeconomic factors prevented us from creating a "clinically perfect" solution that would fail in real homes—a common trap in medical device design.
